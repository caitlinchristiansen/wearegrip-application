Why this role

I have wanted to work in this specific corner of sport for a long time. Not F1 broadly, the personal brand side of it. The part where a driver's identity becomes something a brand wants to be attached to, and someone has to make sure that relationship is managed well. That is the work I find interesting, and it is the work I have been building toward.

The brands working with wearegrip's drivers are not buying reach. They are buying into something a driver's audience already believes in. That only works when the partnership feels right, and keeping it feeling right is the partner manager's job, across every piece of content, all season.

Gasly at Givenchy. Norris at TUMI. These are cultural fits, carefully placed. The work around these drivers demands taste and proper relationship management, not just deadline tracking.

The role is one athlete client, which means real depth rather than breadth. One driver's brand, one set of partners, managed properly. That is a more interesting job than spreading thin across many, and it is the model I want to work in.

I have spent time building the skills that keep partnerships running well, and I understand the internal coordination side too: keeping brand, content and creative teams aligned so the partner never feels the seams.

The partner manager keeps the partnership feeling earned: every activation, all season, across every part of a driver's world.

How I think about it

01

If a partner has to ask, something has already gone wrong

Good partner management is invisible to the partner. They should always know what is coming, what just landed, and what it delivered, before they think to ask. Proactive communication is what keeps a brand renewing rather than reconsidering.

02

The partner has to fit the driver, not the other way around

A driver's personal brand is built on who they actually are: their personality, their world, their audience. A partner that sits awkwardly inside that erodes it. Part of the job is making sure every activation feels like a natural extension of the driver, not a logo placed on top of them.

03

A signed contract is not a partnership. Activation is where it starts

The moment a brand shows up in a driver's world in the right way, the partnership starts doing what it was supposed to do. Getting there requires close work with the creative team and the kind of attention that means nothing slips.

The world as I see it

Driver personal brands are operating at a level the sport has not seen before.

Five years ago, a driver's off-track presence was mostly team merchandise and a few sponsor posts. What is happening now is different. Gasly is fronting Givenchy fragrance campaigns and attending Louis Vuitton shows. Norris has built the LN store into something with its own collections, its own naming language, its own loyal audience that shows up for every drop. These are not just sponsorship arrangements. They are personal brands with real cultural weight.

What makes the best partnerships work is that they fit who the driver actually is. Norris with TUMI works because he genuinely lives that lifestyle: airports, hotels, transit, every other weekend. Gasly with Givenchy works because his aesthetic and how he carries himself off track is genuinely aligned with what that brand represents. The fit has to be real or the audience sees through it immediately.

The direction this is heading is toward drivers as fully realised personal brands, not just athletes with sponsors. The partner manager's job sits right at the centre of that: keeping every brand that comes into a driver's world feeling like it belongs there.

In practice

Building trust when you have no track record to point to.

When Grant Help entered the UK market, we had no existing relationships, no local reputation, and no case studies from that market. Getting partners on board meant convincing people to work with a firm they had never heard of.

What I found was that leading with credentials did not work. Nobody cared about what we had done in Australia. What worked was reframing the conversation entirely around value, specifically making it clear that the way we structured partnerships meant partners only won if clients won. The commercial model did the persuading. My job was to find the right angle for each conversation and make that case clearly and early.

That meant understanding what each potential partner actually cared about, what their own business pressures were, and how to position what we were offering so it answered a real need rather than just describing a service. It was not a script. It was a different framing for every conversation, built on the same underlying logic.

The pipeline we built from that is what I now manage. I think about that experience a lot when I think about what good partner management looks like: the relationship has to be built on something real, or it does not hold.
